Intro

I am Ma. Leonardo Ma. Who am I? I am a designer and programmer. I am Hong Kong-born & Vancouver-raised. As a designer, my direction is influenced by the harmonious structure of Chinese characters, considering every detail of my design and how they can successfully fit together. As a programmer, I am constantly hungry for knowledge. I love getting my hands dirty when tackling challenges.
When I'm in the zone, my approach embodies three qualities of design and coding: meticulousness, omnipresence, and sustainability. By combining the meticulousness in problem solving and making conscious decisions, I strive to maintain sustainable designs and code.

Projects

UrtheCast Co-op

Junior Web Developer & UI Designer

During my 12-month co-op at UrtheCast, a geospatial technology company, I was responsible for UX design, front-end and backend development for the Order Management System department.

Some UX challenges I overcame included providing design solutions when our business model was still under development. It was a challenge that I had resolved through gathering user research and adapting my designs to new findings that impacted our business model. Initially, I was building a public order portal used by anyone. Eventually, we discovered that there could be some sensitivity with sharing geospatial data between countries, thus, requiring management and regulation. I interviewed the stakeholders (UrtheCast customer management team) of this project to find out their needs and work routine. My findings resulted in designs and mockups for a customer-centric view and order-centric view on our platform that allowed our team to successfully manage customer communication and general order management.

In addition to UX design, I was responsible for front-end development by implementing my designs onto the platform using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. As for back-end development, I was amongst the leading team that used Postgres along with spatial database extender, postGIS to build our database in SQL to support geospatial information. For this project, I explored many Python libraries and wrote RESTful APIs, which helped pave the path for exploring this relatively undiscovered field of backend development.

IAT355 Data Visualization

Designer & Developer

For a data visualization course at SFU, I decided to design and build an interactive interface to help users choose their preferred cereal based on price, nutrition, and performance. My teammate and I conducted group interviews to gather user requirements. There were many nutrition and lifestyle factors involved with choosing a cereal. A challenge was to simplify interactions, while showing large amounts of meaningful data. In order to offer sorting based on nutrition, our initial idea was to generate multiple graphs for each nutritional factor. However, the graphs would clutter the view and would be hard for users to focus in on a type of cereal. Instead, I designed a dropdown list of nutrition elements to sort the results on a histogram.

Another important decision made was to Implement a parallel coordinate graph. This was done to maximize the visibility of the data and improve filtering of data based on nutrition values. Also, interaction in one graph would also induce changes in the other two graphs too. For example, selecting a bar in the histogram would highlight a point at the scatterplot and a line in the parallel coordinates respectively and vice versa. The remaining results on the scatterplot and histogram would also be trimmed down based on the range in the parallel coordinate. This project was successful and is still used as an example for the course.

Everyday Design Studio

Research Assistant

I worked as a research assistant at the SFU Everyday Design Studio through qualifying for the Undergraduate Student Research Award. Notable projects that I worked on include the Tilting Bowl, the Hook, and Morse Things. My roles varied from experimenting with different sensors, bridging the front-end and backend logic, version control, programming the front-end mircocontroller for Morse Things, designing the physical mechanism for the Tilting Bowl project, and designing the high-level logic for communication in the Morse Things project.


These projects all stem from the need for consideration, sustainability, and designing within real-world constraints. I heavily worked with circuitry and Arduino (C++) coding, as well as developing high-level communication logic during the Morse Things project. My process includes user research, requirements gathering, and brainstorming. My findings were consolidated and refined into lasting, adaptable solutions, thus reducing the timely cost of changing programming logic in the future.
